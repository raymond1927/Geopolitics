The 2021 Myanmar Coup

A Data-Driven Analysis of a Nation's Democratic Reversal

The Flashpoint: An Undisputed Landslide

The immediate catalyst for the coup was the November 2020 general election. The National League for Democracy (NLD) won a staggering victory, a result the military refused to accept, citing unsubstantiated claims of widespread fraud. This section visualizes the electoral reality versus the military's pretext.

2020 Parliamentary Seat Distribution

The NLD secured approximately 83% of the contested seats, an even larger mandate than in 2015. The military's proxy party, the USDP, won a mere 7%, a result that was widely verified by domestic and international observers as legitimate.

Deep Roots of Power: The Tatmadaw's Constitutional Fortress

The coup was not just about one election. It was the activation of a power structure meticulously designed by the military itself. The 2008 Constitution created a "disciplined democracy" where the Tatmadaw's authority was guaranteed, making any genuine democratic challenge an existential threat to their dominance.

Personal Stakes & Economic Imperatives

Beyond institutional power, the coup was driven by the personal and financial interests of its leaders. For Senior General Min Aung Hlaing, an impending retirement and potential accountability for war crimes created a powerful personal motive, while the military's vast economic empire was at risk under a reforming civilian government.

The coup preempted Min Aung Hlaing's retirement, preserving his authority and shielding him from potential prosecution for alleged war crimes against the Rohingya.

Protecting Military Conglomerates

Billions at Stake

The Tatmadaw controls vast business conglomerates with interests in everything from jade and rubies to banking and tourism. A fully democratic government threatened this lucrative economic empire with transparency and anti-corruption reforms, providing a powerful financial incentive for the coup.

The Catastrophic Aftermath: A Nation in Crisis

The consequences of the coup have been devastating, plunging Myanmar into a full-fledged civil war and reversing a decade of socio-economic progress. The humanitarian and economic costs continue to mount, creating a multifaceted "polycrisis" that grips the nation.

Economic Collapse: GDP Growth & Poverty Rate

The economy contracted sharply by nearly 18% in 2021. Poverty has more than doubled, with nearly half the population now living below the poverty line, wiping out years of economic development.
